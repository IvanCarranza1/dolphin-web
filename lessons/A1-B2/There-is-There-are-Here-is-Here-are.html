There is / There are; Here is / Here are

  1. We use There is / There are to indicate that someone or something exists.

Short answers

Is there…?

Isn’t there…?

Yes, there is. / No, there isn’t.

Are there…?

Aren’t there…?

Yes, there are. / no, there aren’t.

 

There is is followed by a noun that is singular or uncountable. There are is followed by a plural noun. Uncountable and plural nouns can have an indefinite adjective like some or a lot before them. In negative sentences and in questions, any is used instead.

There’s a lot of traffic on the motorway today.

Is there any butter in the fridge? No, there isn’t any left.

Look! There are some stains on you jacket.

 

  1. In a list, we use There’s… if the first thing is singular.

There’s a sandwich, a bar of chocolate and two apples in my bag.

 

  1. To ask about the quantity or number of things or people, we use questions beginning with How much…? and how many…?

How much flour is there? There’s one kilo.

How many boys are there in your group? There’re only two.

NB: There are three of us. (not: We are three.)

There are four of them. (not: They are four.)

  1. There’s and There are are also used as adverbs of place to indicate someone or something that is at a distance from the speaker. NB: Nouns follow the verb and pronouns precede it.

There is your mum! There she is!

Here is used to indicate someone or something close to the speaker. When something is being handed over, we use the expressions here’s and Here are.

Here you are… ten pounds.

Thanks. And here’s your change.

NB: Questions to ask if someone is there or nor are formed differently:

Is your sister there? (not: is there your sister?)

No, she isn’t here. (not: No, there isn’t.)

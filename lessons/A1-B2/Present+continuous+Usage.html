Present continuous

Usage

 

The present continuous is used:

  1. To say what is taking place in a given moment (for example, when we are talking on our mobile phones, describing a scene or picture). In this case, we often use adverbs or time expressions like now, just / right now, at the moment, at present, which usually come at the end of the sentence.

Look. It’s raining hard.

I’m watching a film right now.

My father’s working in the garden at the moment.

 

  1. To describe an action that has already begun but hasn’t ended, not necessarily at the exact moment you are talking, but at that time.

I’m learning French. (at this time)

She’s taking a course in business English. (and she hasn’t finished it yet)

 

  1. To highlight, with a certain sense of annoyance, something which is done insistently, or which isn’t done, but should be. We use the adverb always with the present continuous in this case.

You’re always talking! (it irritates me)

 

  1. To talk about situations that are temporary or different from what one usually does.

I’m staying with an English family. (at this time, it’s not my normal residence)

This week, I’m working in the morning. (not always, only for this week)

I’m teaching in a private school this year.
 

  1. To talk about future actions that are already foreseen or planned. In this case, we always use an adverb or a time expression like tonight, tomorrow, next week / year.

What are you doing next summer?

I’m going to the mountains.

I’m meeting him at the airport tomorrow at nine o’clock.

I’m starting a new school next year.
